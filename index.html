About

I am a Finance PhD candidate at INSEAD. My work sits at the intersection of empirical asset pricing and institutional demand, with a focus on how demand systems can be brought to asset markets in a way that is both structurally disciplined and empirically credible.

Much of my current research examines how portfolio concentration, effective breadth, and aggregation across heterogeneous mandates affect the estimation and interpretation of asset demand. Using mutual fund holdings and related institutional ownership data, I study how estimated demand curves map into stock-level elasticities, disagreement across investors, and broader equilibrium questions.

More broadly, I am interested in the micro-foundations of market clearing, the empirical content of structural asset demand models, and the link between institutional behavior and cross-sectional asset prices.

Why Assets Aren’t Cereal: Market Granularity and Demand Estimation

Ryan Cheng

This paper argues that several puzzling estimates in structural asset demand arise mechanically from granular portfolios and aggregation across heterogeneous mandates. Using mutual fund holdings, I study how portfolio concentration, effective breadth, and institutional disagreement shape identification and the interpretation of estimated demand curves.
